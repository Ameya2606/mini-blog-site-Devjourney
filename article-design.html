The Art of Minimalist UI

Ameya Bhagwat

Dec 10, 2025 • 6 min read

I've always wondered why some websites look "clean" and others look "cluttered." Turns out, it's not magic—it's just a few simple rules about spacing and color that I'm trying to apply in my own projects.

1. The Psychology of Whitespace

Whitespace (or negative space) is not empty; it is a design element itself. Just as silence creates rhythm in music, whitespace creates structure in layout.

"Perfection is achieved, not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away."

Antoine de Saint-Exupéry

When we crowd elements together, the user feels overwhelmed. By using generous padding (like the py-5 classes in this project), we lower the user's stress levels and increase their focus on the content.

2. Visual Hierarchy & Typography

Users don't read websites; they scan them. A strong visual hierarchy guides the eye instantly to what matters.

  • Scale: The H1 title should be significantly larger than everything else.
  • Contrast: Pure black on white can be too harsh. We use "Dark Grey" (#1f2937) for a softer, more premium reading experience.
  • Typeface: We chose Inter for this project. It has a tall "x-height", making it extremely legible even at small sizes on mobile screens.

3. The 60-30-10 Formatting Rule

Struggling with colors? This timeless interior design rule works perfectly for Web UI:

  • 60% Neutral: The background (Whites, Creams, Light Greys).
  • 30% Secondary: The typography and borders (Dark Greys, Blacks).
  • 10% Accent: The Call-to-Actions (Buttons, Links).

I tried to follow this for my blog layout. Green is my accent color, so I only used it for the important stuff—like the buttons you click.

Design is an iterative process. As I build more projects, I hope to refine my eye for these details further.
